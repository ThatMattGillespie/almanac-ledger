Part I · The State of Peacebuilding

A World in Turmoil: What Global Conflict Trends Tell Us

What the latest global conflict data actually says about where we are headed.

Siri Aas Rustad & Henrik Urdal PRIO / July 2026 / 9 min read

The number of active armed conflicts is higher today than at any point since the Second World War. That single fact reshapes almost every assumption the peacebuilding field has carried for a generation, and it is worth sitting with before we reach for solutions.

Conflict has not simply increased. It has changed shape. The wars that dominate the current data are longer, more fragmented, and more likely to draw in outside states through proxies and materiel than through their own troops. Where a peace process once meant a table with a manageable number of chairs, mediators now face a room that keeps adding seats faster than anyone can fill them.

This matters because the tools we built were designed for a different distribution of violence. The liberal peacebuilding model assumed a war would end, a settlement would hold, and institutions could be rebuilt in the quiet that followed. Increasingly there is no quiet. Settlements fray within months, and the space between war and peace has widened into a condition of its own.

Reading the curve honestly

It is tempting to treat rising conflict counts as proof that prevention has failed. The data does not support that reading. Prevention works quietly, and its successes are invisible by design: the war that did not start leaves no line on the chart. What the numbers show is not the failure of prevention but the growing cost of neglecting it.

The practical implication is uncomfortable. If violence is becoming more chronic, then peacebuilding cannot be a discrete intervention with a start and an end. It has to become a standing capacity, funded and staffed the way we fund public health, present before the crisis and patient long after the cameras leave.

That is a demand on institutions, but it is also a demand on the field's own habits. We measure what is easy to count and call the rest unquantifiable. The entries that follow this one take up that challenge directly, from the evidence base to the frontline practice, and they do not agree with each other. Good. A field that is genuinely learning should be arguing.

The trend line is steep, and it is not turning on its own. But a clear-eyed account of where we are is the first thing any serious response requires, and clarity, at least, is within reach.
